# Player Theory Map

| Chapter | What Should the Player Believe? | Evolving Theory Context |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chapter 1** | The station is a rusty corporate deathtrap. | The player assumes a straightforward narrative about corporate negligence and a poorly maintained deep-sea rig. |
| **Chapter 2** | An automated system failed, leading to Miller's death. | The threat feels entirely environmental and accidental. Miller is viewed as a pure, tragic victim of circumstance. |
| **Chapter 3** | Someone is actively hiding a crime (Murder/Sabotage). | After finding deleted security logs, the player believes they are dealing with a human murderer among the crew (likely David). |
| **Chapter 4** | The crew is corrupt and out for themselves. | Finding out about David's smuggling and Otto's missing supplies convinces the player that everyone is greedy, but the monster is just a side effect. |
| **Chapter 5** | The threat is biological and hunting them. | A second body confirms a monster exists. The player realizes they are dealing with an alien/mutagenic threat, not just a murder mystery. |
| **Chapter 6** | Corporate deliberately dug for the organism. | Meeting Victor and seeing the Quarantine zone shifts the blame to Corporate using the crew as expendable guinea pigs for profit. |
| **Chapter 7** | A trusted ally is a corporate spy. | Medbay clues lead the player to believe Dr. Aris or Victor actively infected the crew on Corporate's orders. Paranoia about the survivors peaks. |
| **Chapter 8** | The organism is intelligent and manipulative. | The power failure and the organism mimicking Miller's voice reveals the true psychological horror. The monster is learning from them. |
| **Chapter 9** | Gideon is a corporate hitman silencing the crew. | Discovering the Omega Protocol convinces the player that Gideon doomed them from the start to cover up the corporation's mess. |
| **Chapter 10** | Arthur is the true villain who started it all. | Finding the drill telemetry shifts the blame to Arthur. The player believes his sabotage was malicious or purely selfish, causing the whole nightmare. |
| **Chapter 11** | Gideon is actually a martyr trying to save Earth. | The climax. The player realizes Gideon isn't evil—he triggered Omega to stop an extinction-level event. The player's entire understanding flips. |
| **Chapter 12** | The organism is trying to escape to the surface. | Structural failure reveals the organism is tearing the station apart to stop the scuttling sequence and breach the surface. |
| **Chapter 13** | We have to save the innocent (despite their flaws). | Escaping requires parsing who deserves to be saved based on the Shames uncovered. The player distinguishes between human frailty and malice. |
| **Chapter 14** | My final choice determines the fate of humanity. | The confrontation at the Bathysphere forces the player to weigh saving themselves vs. exposing the truth vs. securing the contagion. |
| **Chapter 15** | Everyone's small sins caused this apocalypse. | Endings. The player understands that the disaster wasn't just one villain's fault—it was a chain reaction of human shame, fear, and desperation. |
